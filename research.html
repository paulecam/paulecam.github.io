Research

My main research interests are planetary and orbital dynamics; as well as stellar populations. Specifically, the birth and death of stars and how that might affect planetary populations.

AST425: Undergraduate Thesis (Dr. Ronan Kerr, Prof. Christopher Matzner)

The (temporary) title of my thesis is Investigating Star Formation Drivers via Age and Velocity Gradients in SPYGLASS Populations. In other words, I’m working on a SPYGLASS project that uses Gaia data to map how star formation unfolds in space and time across the solar neighbourhood. For about a hundred nearby young stellar associations, we compute stellar ages and 3D motions to look for systematic gradients—patterns where stars get younger or change velocity in a coherent direction. By comparing these gradients from region to region, we can test whether they are produced by common feedback-driven processes or by local, one-off events. This will either reveal a recurring “sequencing” of star formation, or highlight particularly interesting complexes for deeper case studies! Quite exciting stuff!

SURF Project - Summer 2025 (Prof. Hanno Rein)

This past summer, I started working under Prof. Hanno Rein through the Natalia Krasnopolskaia Memorial Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship (SURF) at the University of Toronto’s Department of Physics. SURF is a scholarship-based program that supports undergraduate students as they conduct original research under faculty mentorship.
My project focuses on the development and testing of adaptive timestep algorithms for N-body simulations, using the open-source integrator REBOUND Specifically, I’m implementing and evaluating a new method designed to improve accuracy and efficiency when simulating planetary systems with close encounters, an area where traditional algorithms can struggle. I’ve been comparing this new approach to existing schemes using objects like asteroid Apophis as test cases.
Through this project, I’ve deepened my understanding of numerical methods, algorithm design, and orbital dynamics. It’s been an amazing opportunity to get a hands-on sense of what computational astrophysics looks like in practice.
