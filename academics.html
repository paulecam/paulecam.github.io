Academics Deep Dive

I'm currently completing a double major in Astronomy and Physics, with a minor in Mathematics, at the University of Toronto.

Over the past few years, I've built a strong foundation in both theoretical and practical aspects of the physical sciences, ranging from foundational physics and multivariable calculus to thermal physics, quantum mechanics, and differential equations. I’ve been developing computational and analytical skills through coursework in statistics, programming, and experimental physics, tools that are increasingly central to modern astrophysics. I've also enjoyed exploring interdisciplinary links, such as in Christianity and Science, Drugs and the Brain, or What Went Wrong?, which bridge science, theology, medicine and politics; subjects I’ve always found interest in.

Best courses: Practical Astronomy and Introduction to Astrophysics

Two of the most formative courses in my degree have been AST326: Practical Astronomy and AST320: Introductory Astrophysics. AST326 is a lab-based course that provides essential preparation for research in astronomy and physics. I developed a wide range of practical skills: from operating sophisticated instruments like CCD cameras and spectrographs, to performing data reduction and analysis in Python. I also became familiar with the challenges and quirks of ground-based observing and honed my ability to collaborate with team members and communicate findings through formal lab reports. Throughout the course, we completed six major projects: Photon Statistics, Thermal Radiation and the Statistics of Noise, Astronomical Spectroscopy, Radio Telescopes and Pulsars, Mapping the Milky Way, Supernova Light Curves and the Hubble constant. On the theoretical side, AST320 offered a deep dive into the physical principles that govern the cosmos. Working through derivations and mathematical models in an astrophysical context was both intellectually challenging and deeply satisfying. It helped me connect abstract concepts to the real-world phenomena we observe in space, and was overall an amazing course experience.

Honorable Mention: Non-Program Courses

Beyond my core science coursework, I’ve taken a wide range of electives across the humanities and social sciences. My all-time favourite was Christianity and Science, which revealed how deeply the history of science is interwoven with questions of faith. Far from being in conflict, the two have often walked hand in hand, a perspective that challenged the way I think about scientific inquiry. I also loved What Went Wrong?, a political science course exploring key historical moments through the lens of Cassandras, History’s ignored whistleblowers. Rigorous in its theoretical framing, it gave me a newfound appreciation for political science as a discipline grounded in logic and structure. These classes reminded me that science doesn’t happen in a vacuum, it’s always part of a bigger human story; hence they are given the shoutout they deserve here.
